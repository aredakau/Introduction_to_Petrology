Introduction to Metamorphic Facies

  • Definition: Metamorphic facies are groups of minerals stable under specific pressure (P) and temperature (T) conditions.
  • Importance: Essential for understanding metamorphic history, tectonic settings, and geological processes.
  • Usage: Identified by index minerals and mineral reactions unique to each facies.

Source: Winter, J. (2014)

Facies of Low-Pressure Thermal or Contact Metamorphism

  • Characteristic: High temperature, low pressure, often near magma intrusions.
    • Albite-Epidote Hornfels Facies
    • Hornblende Hornfels Facies
    • Pyroxene Hornfels Facies
    • Sanidinite Facies

Pyroxene Hornfels Facies

  • Definition: High-temperature, low-pressure facies typical in contact metamorphism.
  • P-T Range: 600-800Â°C, 1-3 kbar.
  • Index Minerals:
    • Pyroxene (high-temperature stability)

    • Minor Garnet and Hornblende (variable based on composition)

Sanidinite Facies

  • Definition: Highest-temperature facies in contact metamorphism.
  • P-T Range: 800-1000Â°C at very low pressures.
  • Index Minerals:
    • K-feldspar (Sanidine)
    • Cordierite
    • Wollastonite: Forms in calcium-rich rocks under high temperatures.
    • Tridymite: High-temperature silica polymorph.
    • Anorthite: Calcium-rich plagioclase feldspar, stable at high temperatures.
    • Minor Spinel and Sillimanite
    • Quartz is often absent due to extreme conditions.

Facies of Intermediate-Pressure Regional Metamorphism

  • Characteristics: Moderate temperatures and pressures, typical in regional metamorphism.
    • Zeolite Facies: Initial metamorphism, hydrated zeolite minerals.
    • Prehnite-Pumpellyite Facies: Transitional facies with Prehnite and Pumpellyite.
    • Greenschist Facies: Green minerals like Chlorite and Actinolite; moderate temperature.
    • Amphibolite Facies: Hornblende, Plagioclase, and Garnet; high-temperature facies.
    • Granulite Facies: High-temperature, low-fluid conditions with Pyroxenes and Feldspars.

Zeolite Facies

  • Definition: Low-temperature, low-pressure facies found in regional metamorphism, often at the beginning stages of burial metamorphism.
  • P-T Range: 50-200Â°C, 2-4 kbar.
  • Index Minerals: Zeolites (e.g., laumontite, heulandite)

Prehnite-Pumpellyite Facies

  • Definition: Transitional facies between Zeolite and Greenschist.
  • P-T Range: 200-350Â°C, 2-6 kbar.
  • Index Minerals: Prehnite, Pumpellyite, often associated with chlorite and albite.

Greenschist Facies

  • Definition: A low- to moderate-temperature facies formed at intermediate pressures, with distinctive green minerals.
  • P-T Range: 350-500Â°C, 3-10 kbar.
  • Index Minerals: Chlorite, Actinolite, Epidote, Albite.

Granulite Facies

Blueschist Facies

  • Definition: A high-pressure, low-temperature facies characteristic of subduction zones. Named for the presence of blue-colored minerals.
  • P-T Range: 200-500Â°C, 5-16 kbar.
  • Index Minerals:
    • Glaucophane: A blue amphibole, indicating high-pressure stability.
    • Lawsonite: Hydrous mineral stable at high pressures.
    • Minor Jadeite and Epidote: Common in certain compositions.

Eclogite Facies

  • Definition: Ultra-high-pressure facies with moderate to high temperatures, marking the conditions of deeply subducted oceanic crust.
  • P-T Range: 500-800Â°C, >12 kbar.
  • Index Minerals:
    • Omphacite: High-pressure pyroxene stable under eclogite conditions.
    • Garnet: Dense, high-pressure mineral often in large crystals.
    • Minor Kyanite and Rutile: Found in aluminum-rich compositions.

Summary

  • Metamorphic Facies: Groups of minerals that form under specific P-T conditions.
  • Categories: Low-Pressure, Intermediate-Pressure, and High-Pressure.
  • Index Minerals: Key to identifying facies and understanding metamorphic environments.
  • Importance: Provides insight into tectonic settings and geological processes.
